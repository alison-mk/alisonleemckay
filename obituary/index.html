The Obituary

Premise:
On August 29, 2014 Sher Kung was killed in a collision with a truck while bicycling to work. The accident took place when a driver turned left across a painted bike lane on 2nd Avenue in downtown Seattle.

Like hundreds of morning bike commuters, I rode past the scene of the accident and witnessed the aftermath: a mangled bike, a white sheet covering a shape on the pavement, a cyclist’s foot exposed.

Prior to the accident, I had commuted to work via this bike lane every day for a year. Terrified but undeterred, I did not change my route to work for over a year.

Soon after Sher Kung’s death, a piecemeal obituary began forming in my mind for my own potential death in the 2nd Avenue bike lane. New phrases and lines materialized each day I rode to work. This process was unintentional at first, but it eventually became morbidly deliberate.

--

She really loved biking.

--

She wanted a city full of bike lanes.

--

She wanted people to feel safe in the lanes; summer tourists, commuters in the rain, families on weekends.

--

The more drivers she told personally not to turn on a red light, the safer it would be.

--

Would it be?

--

Would the friend remember next time she drove down 2nd Avenue? Or the coworker who listened but seemed distracted?

--

How does a city learn to follow new rules? How do we create the city we want to live in?

--

The presence of bikes in the lane reminds cars to be careful.

This was her mantra.

--

She tried not to yell at the cars.

Especially when their windows were closed.

--

She tried not to get angry, tried not to gesture incoherently at oblivious drivers.

--

She consulted friends about what kinds of gestures might make sense to a driver, versus gestures that might look simply like excited waving.

--

She wrote to the city with ideas for improving safety in the bike lane, like adding signs at parking garage entrances and making the protected left turn lights more prominent.

--

The city did not respond, but the city did post traffic safety officers to protect cyclists at the most hazardous intersections.

--

She recognized other 2nd Avenue bike commuters. They nodded and smiled at stop lights.

--

A community of bicyclists endured, committed to this route.

--

She saw other cyclists shout at cars, defending their space, hearing fear alongside anger in their voices.

--

Yet she watched cyclists run the same red lights as cars, and she ran these lights herself.

--

If there was no traffic, did it matter?

--

How does a community choose not to follow certain rules? How do we shape our communities?

--

Three months, six months, and then a year passed.

--

She witnessed another bicyclist get hit by a car turning illegally across the 2nd Avenue bike lane. He bled but walked away.

------

The city continues to improve the 2nd Avenue bike lane. Cars still endanger cyclists and pedestrians. Cyclists still endanger themselves.

The bicycle, pedestrian and motor vehicle communities endure and evolve, tangled in a complicated dance, vying for space, ownership and awareness.
